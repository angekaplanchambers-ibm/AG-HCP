# Meridian TF Pattern Integration Workshop - Summary and Priority Recommendations

**Date:** July 8, 2026
**Prepared by:** Ange Kaplan-Chambers
**Distribution:** Workshop attendees + original kickoff stakeholders + leadership

## What this document is - and what it is not

This workshop was not designed to produce design solutions or engineering solutions. It was designed to produce categorical directions and a prioritized recommendation set to bring to leadership.

The primary output of this workshop is: here is what matters most, in order. The next step is for leadership to select the initiative that comes first. After that decision is made, design and engineering direct the specific feature, interface, or implementation.

This summary also includes one concrete recommended solution captured offline after the workshop - the immersive experience concept described below. This recommendation is included because it directly addresses the highest-priority workshop output (trust/visibility/governance) and carries a potentially favorable effort-to-impact ratio, pending engineering validation. It is presented alongside the categorical directions, not as a replacement for the leadership decision still required.

---

## How the workshop was run

Participants were not shown Meridian patterns before or during the session. This was deliberate. The goal was to find out whether a cross-functional group - field, engineering, design, and UX research - would independently arrive at the same patterns Meridian already implements, without being primed.

They did. The significance of this is covered below under Meridian Validation.

The session ran in three phases:
1. **Pain point identification** - participants surfaced user pain, workflow friction, design challenges, and agentic obstacles from their own experience and customer contact.
2. **Solution ideation** - participants identified agentic interaction patterns they have observed or want to see across any product.
3. **Priority matrix** - problem categories and solution patterns were matched and ranked: high priority (act now), medium priority (get started), low priority (long tail), not a priority (defer or deprioritize).

## Priority matrix results

### High priority - act now

**Trust / context / visibility / governance**

This cluster received the clearest high-priority signal from the group. The underlying goal - named in post-workshop reflection by Gabrielle Azurdia - is returning control to the customer: customers should not be at the mercy of what the agent sets off to do. Verification must be possible along the way, not only at the end result. The specific capabilities that belong here:

- **Ability to review** - before the agent acts and after. What did it read, what did it propose, what was approved. The interaction model must support inspection, pause, redirect, or rejection at any point - not only at the approval gate.
- **Anti-patterns** - surfacing what the agent should not do, and flagging when a user or system action violates known best practices.
- **Contextual starting points** - the agent does not present a blank interface or a generic list of options. It surfaces informed choices derived from the user's current infrastructure state, active context, and history - so that each launching-off point is already calibrated to what is relevant and actionable for that user at that moment.
- **Providing help** - when the agent cannot resolve a problem, it routes the user to the right resource (documentation, support, a human) rather than looping indefinitely.

These four capabilities are also the core interaction patterns Meridian implements. See Meridian Validation below.

**Proactive detection and observability**

The second high-priority cluster. HCP Terraform customers have virtually no proactive capability today - this is the current-state gap, not a future aspiration. Customers who can prevent drift and failures before they happen will look favorably at AI offerings. The value is not that AI is impressive; it is that AI made a bad thing not happen. The current state requires users to discover problems reactively. That is not acceptable as agentic operations increase in scope.

### Borderline / discussed

**Onboarding**

Raised consistently as a problem, but not placed in the high-priority cluster during the matrix exercise. Nicole Mak's observation is relevant here: many customers are not yet at the point where agentic onboarding makes sense. Basic onboarding - getting users to their first successful run, introducing features they do not know exist - is still unsolved for a wide portion of the user base. This problem may need to be addressed before agentic onboarding can be meaningful.

### Deferred

**Visual presentation**

Not placed in the priority matrix by consensus. The group reframed visual presentation as an emotional and relational question - how the product makes users feel, trust, and connect - rather than a visual fix or UI refresh. This is real, but it is not a near-term intervention. Defer.

## What the workshop surfaced

### Research validation

Greg Bender (UX research) presented eight themes synthesized from four research sprints (hundreds of participant hours, platform engineers, security engineers, app developers). The group found that nearly all pain points and opportunity post-its from the session mapped directly into Greg's eight categories. This means the research signal and the practitioner/field signal are converging on the same problem set.

The eight themes, in research-ranked order:
1. Blast-radius visibility before any change
2. Human-in-the-loop is non-negotiable at this stage
3. Intent-driven operations - but only with explainability
4. Audit trails tell what happened, not why
5. Proactive detection, not reactive discovery
6. Upgrading is the #1 source of ongoing toil
7. Self-service requires golden paths, not open-ended interfaces
8. AI trust is earned through transparency
9. Onboarding and time-to-value remain barriers

Themes 1, 2, 4, 5, and 8 (trust, context, visibility, governance, and proactive detection) map directly to the high-priority matrix outcome.

### New concepts that surfaced during session

These concepts were not in prior planning documents. They are design opportunities worth capturing before the next phase begins.

**Session bookmarks and milestones** (Ange Kaplan-Chambers)
Users can hit failure states in an agent session and need a way to revert to a known-good point without starting over. The proposal: named checkpoints in an agent session, referenceable by name, number, or event label. The user (or the system automatically) marks milestones; the user can say "revert to [checkpoint]" or "from [checkpoint] move forward with this approach." More precise than undo, more useful than session history. Addresses context degradation in long sessions.

**Advisor: proactive capability discovery** (Nicole Mak)
Instead of presenting a blank chat prompt, the agent surfaces what it can currently help with on the user's active screen - based on the state of their infrastructure, active runs, or recent activity. The user does not need to know what to ask. The agent tells them what is available. Nicole is already prototyping this concept.

**Agent as teaching tool** (Amy Brown)
When the agent takes an action, it explains what it did, why it did it, and links to the relevant documentation. The user can ignore the explanation, but it is always present. The goal is that users come away understanding how to do things themselves - the agent is not just an errand runner. This also makes AI a search and documentation indexer for users who find HashiCorp docs difficult to navigate.

**Digital twin / reality-check gate** (Ashley Adamson)
The gate before an agent acts on production should not be another agent reviewing the output and saying "that looks right." The gate should be: does this actually match reality? Ashley's framing: the Terraform plan and apply process may already be this gate, or something better may be needed. This is a question for engineering.

### Micro-interaction design is a separate workstream

Greg Bender raised that small broken interactions erode trust more than large failures. Example: pressing the up arrow in a chat input field triggers history navigation instead of moving the cursor; on return, all user formatting (carriage returns) has been stripped. These failures are low-visibility but high-impact on user trust. Micro-interaction design for the agentic surface needs its own dedicated pass, separate from the macro flow design work.

## Meridian validation

The workshop hypothesis: Meridian patterns are not Meridian-specific inventions. They are common patterns that practitioners already expect from any credible agentic system. If the workshop could surface these patterns without Meridian priming, the hypothesis would be confirmed.

Result: confirmed.

Ability to review, contextual starting points, anti-patterns, providing help, and human-in-the-loop gates all emerged from the group independently. Greg Bender and Amy Brown had not seen Meridian in a live demo. Nicole Mak and Gabrielle Azurdia had seen screenshots only. The pattern convergence happened without direct exposure.

This means the design shortcut is available: adopt Meridian's existing patterns into the Terraform experience rather than designing from scratch. The workshop validated this approach. This shortcut is relevant to the six-month timeline.

## The call and response experience - existing design work and what it solves

What leadership has been calling "chat" is better described as a **call and response** experience: a structured, back-and-forth interaction between the user and the agent where the user issues a request and the agent responds with an action, a plan, an explanation, or a question. This is distinct from an open-ended chat interface with a blank prompt and no guardrails.

Design ideations for this call and response experience already exist. This is a meaningful advantage. We are not starting from zero on the interaction model - there is prior design work that can be evaluated, pressure-tested against the workshop output, and advanced into the next phase rather than initiated from scratch.

Those existing designs address several of the problems the workshop surfaced. Specifically, the call and response model - as currently ideated - has potential to deliver against:

- **Contextual starting points** - the interaction can be initiated from a specific infrastructure state or problem rather than a blank prompt
- **Ability to review** - a structured call and response naturally produces a reviewable record of what was asked and what the agent proposed
- **Providing help** - the response layer can surface documentation, support routing, and next-step guidance as part of the reply pattern
- **Intent-driven operations with explainability** - the agent explains what it is doing and why as part of the response, not as an afterthought

The open question is not whether to pursue a call and response experience. The question is how much of the highest-priority problem cluster the existing designs already cover, and where the gaps are. That review - mapping the existing design ideations against the workshop priority matrix - is the practical next step for the design team.

This is also a direct counterpart to the immersive experience recommendation. The call and response experience is the lighter-weight, more accessible entry point; the immersive mode is the deeper, permissioned layer for users who need full control. Both can exist in the same product. The call and response designs provide a foundation that the immersive experience builds on.

## Recommended solution: immersive experience - Meridian inside Terraform

*Captured offline after the workshop. Not a workshop output - a post-workshop recommendation for leadership consideration.*

### The concept

Introduce a permissioned "immersive mode" (working name: god mode) within HCP Terraform. A user with the appropriate role enters a full-control experience where they can interact directly with Meridian - either directing the agent to make changes on their behalf, or stepping through a decision tree to solve a specific infrastructure problem with the agent as guide.

The experience is deeper than a chat panel or an advisory widget. It is a dedicated mode: the user consciously enters it, operates with expanded agency and visibility, and exits when the task is complete.

### Why this addresses the highest-priority problem

The workshop identified trust, context, visibility, and governance as the highest-priority cluster. The immersive mode directly delivers all four: the user sees everything the agent is doing, controls the decision points, and operates within a governed, permissioned boundary. It is not passive monitoring - it is active, traceable, human-in-the-loop operation at the level of detail the workshop said customers need most.

It also addresses the "blank interface" problem raised by Amy Brown and Ange: rather than presenting an open-ended prompt, the immersive mode gives the user contextual entry points - here is your current infrastructure state, here are the problems the agent has identified, here is where you can begin.

### The implementation path

Meridian is already built. The proposed integration method is an iFrame embedding Meridian inside the HCP Terraform UI, accessible to users with the appropriate role.

**This is an assumption, not a validated engineering estimate.** The following factors need engineering sign-off before the effort claim can be confirmed:
- Authentication and SSO state across the iFrame boundary
- Content Security Policy (CSP) header compatibility
- Cross-origin restrictions between the Terraform and Meridian domains
- Accessibility compliance (keyboard navigation, screen reader support) within an embedded iFrame
- Performance impact of the embedded surface on the host page

If the iFrame approach clears these checks, the engineering effort is expected to be low relative to building a native integration from scratch. If it does not clear them, the fallback is a deeper native integration, which carries higher engineering cost.

### Effort-to-impact framing

| Dimension | Assessment |
|---|---|
| User impact | High - directly addresses the highest-priority workshop cluster |
| Engineering effort (iFrame path) | Assumed low - pending engineering validation |
| Design effort | Low to medium - Meridian's interaction patterns are already designed |
| Risk | Low if iFrame approach is technically viable; medium if native integration is required |
| Timeline fit | Potentially favorable for the six-month window - no new design system work required |

## Leadership: structure and decisions needed

Prakash Manglanathan raised the PM ownership gap in the first minutes of the session. There is no PM formally assigned to the Meridian-Terraform integration initiative. Paul is investigating the business case. Omar owns the agentic side of Terraform. Design and field have been driving the initiative without a dedicated PM coordinating the customer-facing experience from marketing and demand generation through adoption.

Prakash's concern: teams working toward the same goal in silos. The buying and adoption journey risks being fragmented if product management, engineering, design, and field are not coordinated under a clear owner. This is a structural condition that affects whether the six-month timeline is achievable.

The workshop produced categorical direction. The next step is a leadership decision on which initiative comes first. That decision unlocks the specific design and engineering work.

**The decision needed:** Which priority cluster does the team commit to first - trust/visibility/governance or proactive detection/observability? Or does leadership want to frame this differently?

Once that decision is made:
- Design directs the specific interface and interaction pattern for that initiative.
- Engineering scopes the implementation.
- The Meridian shortcut (adopting existing patterns) is available to accelerate both.
