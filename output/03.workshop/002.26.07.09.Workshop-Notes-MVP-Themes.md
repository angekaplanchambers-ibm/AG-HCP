# MVP Themes — ILM AI / Agentic Infrastructure

**Synthesized from:** Research Sprints 1–4 (Feb–Jun 2026) · Ground Rules · JTBD · AGF-ILM AI Vision & Strategy

---

## How to read this document

Each theme is grounded directly in participant evidence from the research, aligned to the six Jobs to Be Done (JTBDs), and evaluated through the lens of the workshop ground rules (near-term, constrained resources, MVE protection). Themes are ordered by signal strength and MVP relevance — not aspirational priority.

---

## Theme 1 — Blast-Radius Visibility Before Any Change

**One-sentence summary:** Participants across all four sprints cannot confidently understand the full downstream impact of a Terraform module, provider, or workspace version change before applying it — and this is the single most cited source of fear and manual toil.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 4 (James): *"Nothing in the plan said 'this rebuilds your cluster.' A reviewer skimming the MR sees a trivial version bump and misses that it freezes deploys across every prod region."*
- Sprint 4 (Joel): *"It's an important fashion upgrade… you can't go through and update everything. You can't be doing that all the time."*
- Sprint 4 (Janak): *"A major provider upgrade had an undocumented schema change that triggered unexpected resource recreation in a multi-tenant environment."*
- Sprint 4 (Antonin): *"A change that looked like a simple provider upgrade was now touching resources outside the scope we had originally planned for."*
- Sprint 4 (Harish): *"A minor version bump on the azurerm provider silently dropped a diagnostic setting during apply. We only caught it in a post-deployment CSPM review."*
- Sprint 3 (Mohammed Hassan): Version upgrade discovery is entirely manual — spreadsheets across multiple GitHub/GitLab orgs.
- Sprint 3 (Rajendra): *"I make sure to run terraform plan, get the plan reviewed by AI agent as well as my peers, ask agent for checking cyclic dependencies."*
- Sprint 2 (Xavier): Managing multiple state files creates complexity that Terraform Cloud could abstract.

**Alignment:** JTBD 1 (Understand my estate), JTBD 2 (Safely change infrastructure from intent), JTBD 3 (Remediate operational problems)

**MVP direction:** An AI-powered blast-radius analyzer that, given a proposed module/provider/workspace version change, surfaces: (1) all affected workspaces and consumers, (2) the expected plan diff with risk annotations, (3) any known breaking changes from provider changelogs, (4) recommended human review checkpoints. This is the "Option A → B" progression participants described in Sprint 4 Day 3.

## Theme 2 — Governance, Identity, and Human-in-the-Loop Are Non-Negotiable

**One-sentence summary:** Every participant across every sprint drew the same hard line: AI agents can assist, analyze, draft, and propose — but production changes require explicit human approval, least-privilege identity scoping, and a full audit trail.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 4 (James): *"The danger isn't the agent doing something obviously destructive, it's removing the human pause in front of a change whose risk the plan understates."*
- Sprint 4 (Janak): *"Agents never get permanent keys. They use short-lived, just-in-time (JIT) tokens via OIDC or Vault… InfoSec and our architecture teams set the guardrails."*
- Sprint 4 (Antonin): *"Reviews tended to focus on the new permission being requested rather than asking, 'If we look at everything this agent can do today, is this still the right security boundary?'"*
- Sprint 4 (Harish): *"We do not trust agents in changing the infrastructure codes and stuff… We are testing it in an isolated environment."*
- Sprint 4 (Joel, Day 3): *"Option B (Draft it): Agent bumps the version, runs the plan, rewrites config, and opens a PR"* — the most commonly selected autonomy level.
- Sprint 3 (Shiladitya, BFSI): *"Final production approvals, regulatory compliance sign-offs, and strategic architecture decisions should not be fully outsourced to AI."*
- Sprint 3 (Mohammed): *"If I don't understand the code or the system that I'm dealing with… I wouldn't delegate it because I feel like then I can't actually check."*
- Sprint 1 (Agentic AI session, Felipe): *"We limited the quantity of actions… concerned about data leaks… not using for critical applications yet."*

**Alignment:** JTBD 4 (Govern autonomous infrastructure work), JTBD 2 (Safely change from intent)

**MVP direction:** Every agentic action must be observable (tool trace, approval state, identity), scoped to least privilege (short-lived credentials, OIDC/Vault JIT), and require explicit human approval before touching production. The Trust Ladder model from the Vision doc (L1 → L4) should be surfaced in the UX, enabling customers to incrementally adopt higher autonomy. MCP emerged as the preferred integration protocol for governance consistency (Sprint 4: Antonin, Adama, Harish, Rajendra).

## Theme 3 — Intent-Driven Operations Reduce Toil Without Replacing Expertise

**One-sentence summary:** Participants are ready to tell the system *what* they want to achieve and let AI figure out the Terraform steps — but only when the system can explain its reasoning, reference authoritative sources, and produce a reviewable plan.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 4 (Adama, on Codex): *"I use it much more as a collaborative agent… significantly better at understanding the context of an entire repository, following templatized patterns."*
- Sprint 4 (Rajendra): *"I use them in planning mode and then implementation mode after clarifying everything."*
- Sprint 3 (Atul): *"If we get just the recommendations without any explaining or an official documentation… we can't trust the AI's response."*
- Sprint 3 (Jay): *"AI can parse logs and identify anomalies and suggest potential issues… troubleshooting with AI has been a game-changer."*
- Sprint 3 (Shiladitya): AI performs *"intelligent log correlation, root cause analysis, predictive infrastructure capacity analysis, automated anomaly detection"* — but humans make final decisions.
- Sprint 2 (Lavanya, on blueprints): *"You set what the resource can look like and you can… the teams can just adapt it, so there is a standardization."*
- Sprint 2 (Xavier, on AI-assisted Terraform): *"We have built like agents.md, the starting point of the prompt… all they have to do is add some prompt and it will call the verified module."*
- Sprint 1 (Platform Engineers): Unanimous: AI reduces time on log triage, script generation, troubleshooting — but architecture and governance decisions stay human.

**Alignment:** JTBD 2 (Safely change from intent), JTBD 5 (Scale expert workflows)

**MVP direction:** A chat/intent interface that translates natural language infrastructure goals into Terraform plan previews with cited reasoning and references. This matches the "AGX Chat" roadmap item (Q1 2027) but the MVP should start narrow: intent → plan → human-reviewed apply for 3–5 top workflows (upgrades, new workspace, drift remediation).

## Theme 4 — Drift, Failures, and Anomalies Need Proactive Detection — Not Reactive Discovery

**One-sentence summary:** Participants consistently manage drift, failed runs, and configuration anomalies reactively. They want AI to surface these issues before they become incidents, with enough context to act — not just alert.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 3 (Vinay K): *"We are heavily using AI agent workflow in anomaly detection and event co-relation… AI tools have heavily helped in correlating events from various sources."*
- Sprint 3 (Divya): *"AI-assisted tools help identify unusual behavior, detect risks faster, and reduce alert noise."*
- Sprint 3 (Shiladitya): *"AI-driven anomaly detection significantly improves proactive monitoring capabilities… Faster mean time to resolution (MTTR), reduced alert fatigue."*
- Sprint 3 (Mohammed Hassan): *"AI can handle alert correlation, detect anomalies, suggest initial root causes… I still validate AI outputs, any complex edge cases."*
- Sprint 4 (Harish): Agent automatically scans Terraform files against CIS/NIST controls, flags findings mapped to file paths and line numbers — *"Nothing sits in a log somewhere waiting to be noticed."*
- Sprint 4 (Joel): Observability Agent *"checks several disparate data sources, cross references them… has become one of my go-to tools for incident response research."*
- Sprint 2 (Platform Engineers): AI for log analysis, pattern detection, and root cause suggestion is already in use — AI for autonomous remediation is not yet trusted.

**Alignment:** JTBD 1 (Understand my estate), JTBD 3 (Remediate operational problems)

**MVP direction:** An estate health view that continuously surfaces drift, failed runs, policy violations, cost anomalies, and upgrade risks in a single pane. AI proposes remediation steps with blast-radius context. The system suggests; the human approves and applies. This directly supports the "AGX Agent: Analyzer" roadmap item (Q3 2026).

## Theme 5 — Upgrade Lifecycle Is the #1 Source of Ongoing Toil

**One-sentence summary:** Module, provider, and Terraform workspace version upgrades are nearly universal pain — slow, manual, high-risk, and disproportionately time-consuming relative to the business value delivered.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 3 (Lada, synthesis observation): *"It takes 6 to 9 months to upgrade. I have 400 plus GitHub modules. That's a labor cost. It's a time cost."*
- Sprint 3 (Mohammed Hassan): Upgrade process requires manual repo audits across GitLab and GitHub, spreadsheets, change management meetings, team-wide effort.
- Sprint 4 (James): *"I'd say it's quite manual in terms of needing to read through changelogs, etc., online and documentation."*
- Sprint 4 (Joel): *"You can't go through and update everything. You can't be doing that all the time. You need a lot of manpower."*
- Sprint 4 (Joel, on agentic solution): *"I've privately worked on an agentic solution that checks for new provider versions, opens a PR, and makes the version bump itself. It would save real toil."*
- Sprint 4 (Day 3 survey): Majority preference is **Option B** — *"Agent bumps the version, runs the plan, rewrites config, and opens a PR"* — with Option C (auto-PRs for clean plans) as the near-future goal.
- Sprint 4 (Janak): Actively training agents to automate blast-radius impact mapping before merging.

**Alignment:** JTBD 2 (Safely change from intent), JTBD 5 (Scale expert workflows), JTBD 6 (Onboard and expand)

**MVP direction:** An AI-assisted upgrade workflow: (1) detect outdated providers/modules across all workspaces, (2) analyze breaking changes from changelogs, (3) map blast radius, (4) draft updated `.tf` files, (5) open a PR with a pre-computed plan for human review. Human approves merge and apply. This is the core use case driving Sprint 4 research and maps directly to the "AGX Agent: Upgrade" roadmap item (Q4 2026).

## Theme 6 — Self-Service Infrastructure Requires Golden Paths, Not Open Playgrounds

**One-sentence summary:** App developers and less-experienced engineers need pre-approved, standardized infrastructure templates (blueprints) they can parameterize and deploy — reducing platform team bottlenecks while preserving governance.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 2 (Lavanya, on blueprints): *"You set what the resource can look like… teams can just adapt it, so there is a standardization."*
- Sprint 2 (Fahmi, on existing developer portal): *"It's already working for my organization… developers can take templates and deploy their own stack."*
- Sprint 2 (Dennis): *"Moving from traditional DevOps support to building Internal Developer Platforms… self-service platforms so developers can deploy and manage applications on their own."*
- Sprint 2 (Xavier): AI-generated Terraform from standardized prompts works well; the concern is integration with existing GitHub-first workflows.
- Sprint 1 (Antonio): *"Build reusable Infrastructure as Code modules, EKS blueprints… golden CI/CD pipelines."*
- Sprint 1 (Mikolaj): Standardizing via golden paths reduces variance and defect rates.
- Sprint 1 (Lavanya): *"Teams now have platform ready with teams focusing on application development solely."*
- Sprint 1 (CloudOps Managers): Ticket-based infrastructure requests are common; self-service with guardrails is the direction all orgs are moving.

**Alignment:** JTBD 5 (Scale expert workflows), JTBD 6 (Onboard and expand)

**MVP direction:** Blueprint-based self-service (already partially shipped as the developer portal/blueprints feature in HCP Terraform). Key gaps: (1) must integrate cleanly with GitHub as source of truth, (2) AI assistance for filling blueprint parameters from natural language intent, (3) plan preview before provisioning. The Sprint 2 demo revealed that participants valued the concept but required VCS integration to trust and adopt it.

## Theme 7 — AI Trust Is Earned Incrementally Through Transparency and Explainability

**One-sentence summary:** Participants do not resist AI — they resist AI they cannot verify. Trust grows when AI shows its work, cites sources, admits uncertainty, and stays within defined boundaries.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 3 (Atul): *"If we get just the recommendations without any explaining or an official documentation or list of documentations… we can't trust the AI's response."*
- Sprint 3 (Jay): *"Don't take AI hypothesis as a fact and just run with it, test/verify first… Give meaningful context to AI."*
- Sprint 3 (Mohammed Hassan): *"I try to look if the response is technically correct and consistent… I try to get references if possible to ensure it isn't being confidently wrong."*
- Sprint 4 (Antonin): *"Traditional monitoring tells us what happened, but not always why a particular action was taken, what context was used, or whether the decision aligned with the intended policy."*
- Sprint 4 (Adama): *"I still validate plans and review changes… I do trust it more than I did a year ago because of these enhancements, but it's blind trust."*
- Sprint 1 (Mark Fisher on GitHub Copilot): *"It took several months for us to trust it because we were working with older models that tended to hallucinate."*
- Sprint 1 (Ishaan, synthesizing): *"What makes a safe, trustworthy agent… what is stopping people from adopting agents currently."* — The core research question from day one.

**Alignment:** JTBD 4 (Govern autonomous infrastructure work), all JTBDs as a cross-cutting concern.

**MVP direction:** Every AI-proposed action must include: (1) what it will do and why, (2) what it consulted (changelogs, provider docs, policy), (3) what it is uncertain about, (4) a human-reviewable plan before execution. The system should surface confidence levels and flag when the agent is operating at the edge of its knowledge. This is table stakes for regulated-environment adoption (BFSI, healthcare, government customers represented in all four sprints).

## Theme 8 — Onboarding and Time-to-Value Remain Barriers to Adoption and Expansion

**One-sentence summary:** New Terraform adopters and teams expanding usage face steep learning curves around module selection, import/migration, policy setup, and workspace organization — and AI has yet to meaningfully accelerate this path.

**Evidence across sprints:**
- Sprint 1 (Ideal Onboarding sessions, March 6): Multiple participants described onboarding as a bottleneck — relying on tribal knowledge, documentation gaps, and waiting for platform team support.
- Sprint 3 (Lada, Manage Identities session): *"Terraform, the product is expensive… it takes 6 to 9 months to upgrade. I have 400 plus GitHub modules."* — upgrade friction discourages expansion.
- Sprint 2 (Self-Service session): Developers using community edition skip the HCP Terraform UI entirely once infrastructure is deployed, reducing visibility into onboarding paths.
- Sprint 4 (Vikas): *"Before making changes to shared modules or upgrading providers, I typically review the Terraform plan, validate changes in lower environments."* — informal onboarding patterns persist.
- Sprint 4 (Janak): AI for *"Workflow Optimization: Automating developer environment setups"* is in use — but still custom-built per org.
- Sprint 1 (Slack — all cohorts): The absence of standardized onboarding templates, module registries, and best-practice guidance forces each team to reinvent the wheel.

**Alignment:** JTBD 6 (Onboard and expand Terraform usage faster)

**MVP direction:** AI-guided onboarding for new workspace creation, import of unmanaged resources, and first-run module selection. This supports the "AGX Agent: Import" roadmap item (Q1 2027). The near-term MVP opportunity is reducing time-to-first-successful-run by surfacing the right module, the right configuration, and the right policy guardrail — without requiring a platform engineer in the loop.

## Cross-Cutting MVP Constraints (from Ground Rules)

The following constraints from the workshop ground rules shape how each theme translates to MVP scope:

| Ground Rule | Implication for MVP |
|---|---|
| **Optimize for the next 6 months** | Themes 1, 2, and 5 (blast radius, governance, upgrade lifecycle) are the most near-term solvable. Themes 3, 7, and 8 require more foundational AI quality. |
| **Assume resource constraints are real** | One consolidated agentic UX surface is preferable to per-team agent sprawl. AGX Platform must be the shared substrate. |
| **Protect the MVE** | The plan → human-approve → apply loop is the core experience that must never be bypassed in the MVP. |
| **Anchor in user and business value** | All themes are grounded in real participant pain (sprint evidence above) and map to JTBD outcomes (speed, safety, scale). |
| **Prioritize decisions over discovery** | The workshop should resolve: which 3–5 workflows enter the MVP scope first, and which Trust Ladder level (L1–L3) is the initial target. |
| **Discuss tradeoffs explicitly** | Autonomy vs. control is the central tradeoff. Sprint 4 data shows users favor Option B (agent drafts, human approves) over Option C (auto-close) for now. |
