## Key Discussion Points

### 1. Why this workshop exists

Multiple teams across the org are running independent AI initiatives. Everyone is moving fast but in different directions. The risk: teams talk to each other too much trying to figure out what others are doing, and nothing actually gets built.

Ange's framing: the workshop is not to figure out the roadmap (done) or the direction (done, via conversations with Paul and Ashley on Meridian). The workshop is to answer: **what do we do right now, this week, and what do we build first.**

The decision to hyperfocus on Meridian pattern integration into TFE is based on Paul and Ashley's Meridian work already being in a good place and being packaged with TFE for Z. Revenue opportunity is real and immediate. The right move is to close the gap between TFE and Meridian by adopting the patterns Meridian has already built.

### 2. Time pressure

- "Six months" is how the initiative is framed externally.
- Design and strategy effectively have three months - they were already in late June.
- Target for having something visible: HashiConf (October) was mentioned, December as the outer bound.
- The goal is not a finished product. It is: something that shows "yes, we are agentic, we are addressing the pain points, we are moving forward." Not watching agents drive churn from the sidelines.

### 3. Marc's definition questions (and answers)

These questions surfaced important gaps that the workshop brief and "what if" scenarios need to close.

**What is a pattern?**
Ange's definition: an established micro-interaction or visual feature that has already been built into a piece of software and can be lifted and applied elsewhere. Either experiential (a workflow, a call-and-response loop, a decision trail) or visual (an interactive panel, a chat surface). Examples: the back-and-forth call/response loop in VS Code, Bob, Copilot.

**What is MVE?**
Minimum Viable Experience. Includes design solutions, interactive patterns, and the overall feeling of the experience. Not a feature list - the experience as a whole.

**How does Meridian differ from AGX?**
Ange's answer: Meridian is the interactive layer - the thing the user sees and interacts with. AGX is the backend orchestration platform. Meridian gives a layer of abstraction between the user and the agent - it generalizes the request, handles checks and balances, makes things crystal clear before acting, and handles complex multi-step workflows that standard LLMs don't manage well. AGX is what coordinates and triggers agents; Meridian is what the user experiences.

Gabrielle added: TFE first (packaged with Meridian for Z), then TFC (Meridian capabilities under the hood from other initiatives).

### 4. Ange's TFE product vision

Not source-verified by user feedback, but stated clearly: TFE should become a **monitoring and observability platform**. Not a place where users make changes and decisions directly.

"Trophies on a mantle. What has TFE automatically done for you? What problems has it solved for you? Not a laundry list of things we need to give you."

This frames the agentic TFE experience as: you see the rewards of your infrastructure working, the agents handling the low-level work, and you are in a position of oversight and confidence - not in a position of constant manual execution.

Implication: write actions, production changes, and destructive operations belong behind approval gates and should be exceptional, not routine. The front end shows evidence of autonomous work and routes exceptions to humans.

### 5. Trust ladder

Multiple participants converged on this:
- Users will never want autonomous production apply. That is probably not a goal for this window.
- Micro-automations first - build trust incrementally.
- The front end of TFE is better suited as an observability and confidence surface than as an execution surface.
- Trust is earned through visibility: show what the agent read, what it proposed, what was approved. Don't hide the work.

This is consistent with strategy doc 002's approval gate design and audit record requirements.

### 6. How Meridian handles complexity

Ange: Meridian is more sophisticated than a standard LLM chat interface. It ensures things are crystal clear before acting - you have vetted what it is about to do, then it proceeds. Designed for complex workflows. Narrows the decision tree as it goes: shows a summary of activities along the way, absconds the things you didn't select, shows the process from start to finish in a summarized form. Doesn't throw everything at you - gives you direct, in-context information.

Marc's framing: "like I give you something, it passes back, I verify, I give you something else, pass it back - very chatty interaction vs. throw it over the fence and expect it done."

### 7. Workshop structure confirmed

| Segment | Attendees | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Kickoff | All stakeholders | Align on direction, surface patterns |
| Working session | Designers + engineering leads | Evaluate specific Meridian patterns, assess effort, make decisions on what to adopt |
| Final readout | Everyone | Present insights, confirm direction, determine featurettes and engineering effort |

The working session is deliberately narrow: designers and engineering leads evaluating specific patterns that can be replicated from Meridian into TF, assessing design decisions and engineering effort together.

### 8. Why Meridian patterns are the right starting point

Meridian has already adopted patterns that are common across the industry: VS Code, Bob, Copilot all use the same back-and-forth call/response loop with interactive panels and in-context information. These patterns are known to work.

If Meridian is being packaged with Terraform, they need to look and feel related. Adopting Meridian patterns into TF closes that gap, builds user trust in agent workflows, signals responsiveness to customer complaints about missing agent support in the front end, and gives a replicable pattern set to start from.
