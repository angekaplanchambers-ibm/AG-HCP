# 021.26.07.08.Meridian-TF-Pattern-Integration-Workshop-Synthesis

## TL;DR

Cross-functional workshop (design, field, engineering, UX research) to identify which agentic design patterns are most important to bring into HCP Terraform. Participants arrived at the same patterns Meridian implements - without being shown Meridian first. Four findings drive the next step:

1. **Trust, context, and visibility are the highest-priority cluster.** Ability to review, anti-patterns, contextual starting points, and governance all converge here. This is where design work should start.
2. **Proactive detection and observability are high priority.** Not a distant vision - customers need it now and it appeared independently across all participant groups.
3. **"Chat" is not yet validated as the right answer.** Leadership keeps reaching for chat as the solution. The workshop did not confirm that chat solves the highest-priority problem. This needs to be stated explicitly in the output summary.
4. **Meridian pattern validation is complete.** The workshop was structured so participants could not be primed by Meridian patterns. They called out ability to review, contextual starting points, anti-patterns, and providing help independently. These are Meridian's patterns. This shortcut is now available.

New design concepts surfaced that are not yet in the pipeline: session bookmarks/milestones (Ange), advisor proactive capability discovery (Nicole), agent-as-teaching-tool (Amy), digital twin reality-check gate (Ashley).

## Key Discussion Points

### 1. PM and leadership structure - unresolved

Prakash raised the absence of a dedicated PM in the first two minutes. Current state: Paul is investigating the business case for Meridian + Terraform on Z. Omar owns the agentic side of Terraform. There is no PM formally assigned to the Meridian-Terraform integration initiative.

Prakash explicitly flagged the silo risk: multiple teams working toward the same customer-facing goal without coordination on the buying and adoption journey. Ashley confirmed Omar and Paul are both in the loop but acknowledged the concern.

This is a structural risk that exists outside the design scope. It is worth naming in the summary to leadership.

### 2. Six-month deadline confirmed

Ange stated the deadline as six months, communicated by leadership. Design needs to intervene even earlier than that to give engineering enough time. Engineering lead for this initiative has not been formally designated - Amy Brown is the TFE engineering representative but not the designated manager.

### 3. JTBDs reviewed as grounding frame

Ange anchored the workshop in the six JTBDs from the AGF ILM AI Vision and Strategy document:
1. Understand your state
2. Safely change infrastructure from intent
3. Remediate operational problems
4. Govern autonomous work
5. Scale expert workflows across teams
6. Onboard and expand TF usage faster

These are the same six JTBDs in `017` (MVP themes synthesis). Participants used these as the working frame for the rest of the session.

### 4. Greg Bender's eight MVP themes independently matched the workshop output

Greg synthesized thousands of pages of UX research sprint data (platform engineers, security engineers, app developers) into eight themes using AI synthesis. The themes, in priority order:

1. Blast-radius visibility - what will this do, beforehand
2. Human-in-the-loop is non-negotiable - nothing in production without it (expected to change over time)
3. Intent-driven operations with explainability - ready to tell us what they want, but only if the system explains its reasoning
4. Audit tells what happened, not why - the why is critical for regulated environments
5. Proactive detection, not reactive discovery
6. Upgrading is the #1 source of toil - users avoid it unless forced to
7. Self-service golden paths - make it easy, remove navigation overhead
8. AI trust earned through transparency and explainability
9. Onboarding and time-to-value remain barriers

Amy Brown confirmed on the spot: almost all post-its from the rest of the group fit into Greg's categories. The group then used Greg's categories as the organizing structure for the rest of the workshop.

This directly validates strategy doc `002` and the `017` MVP themes synthesis. The research-to-workshop signal chain is now closed.

### 5. Ashley Adamson's pain points (field/agentic perspective)

Ashley framed her inputs as agentic problems - she said these are the only Terraform problems she thinks about now. In order of presentation:

- **Cannot set up Terraform agentically from CLI.** Should be able to say "configure Terraform cloud, do X, Y, Z" and have it done. Absence of this is a reason to choose Spacelift instead.
- **Untransparent errors require copy-paste to external agents.** Users copy errors out of HCP TF and paste them into a separate agent. "Why can't I just have this all done within one system?"
- **Wants HashiCorp's Terraform expertise baked into the agent harness.** The value unlock: an agent with a bespoke knowledge harness for your system. Customers have always wanted expertise from the application layer; this is now achievable.
- **Infrastructure should proactively manage itself.** Observability and ongoing state management should not require the user to monitor.
- **HCP TF UI is too complex for new users.** Preference: stay in GitHub, only go to HCP TF when necessary. The agent should be the navigation layer.
- **Digital twin / reality-check gate.** Before an agent acts on production, the gate should not be another agent saying "that looks right" - it should be "does this actually match reality?" The Terraform plan and apply process may be the mechanism, or there may be something better.
- **Troubleshooting goes down rabbit holes.** Tooling does not support the depth of investigation required.

### 6. Nicole Mak's signals

Nicole's customer base over the past year has clustered around cost and security integrations. Her observations:

- Customers need full business context. Platform teams, FinOps teams (who set budgets), and security teams (who set guardrails) are fragmented - they do not know what each other's teams are doing and do not want to take ownership of each other's problems.
- Customer maturity varies widely. Some customers do not know run tasks exist (community edition users). Agentic onboarding is being pushed, but basic onboarding is still unsolved for a large portion of the user base.
- Nicole is prototyping an **advisor concept**: surfacing agent capabilities proactively in the UI before the user knows to ask. Not a blank chat prompt, not a reactive chatbot - the agent surfaces what it can help with on the current screen, so the user can see it without needing to already know what to ask.

### 7. Amy Brown's signals (TFE engineering, AI interaction patterns)

Amy contributed both engineering context and personal AI interaction observations:

- **Customers want to be told where to start.** "They don't want to be presented with an empty screen with an unknown amount of possibilities. They want us to tell them where they should start." The framing: instead of "what do you want to do?" → "here are the things I could be doing for you - which do you want to start with?"
- **Agent-as-teaching-tool.** When the agent does something, it should explain what it did, why, and link to the relevant documentation. Customers should come away understanding how to do it themselves. They can ignore the explanation, but it should always be there.
- **AI as documentation search indexer.** Users have complained about inaccessibility of HashiCorp docs. The agent can serve as a search layer.
- **Direct support channel escalation.** When a problem exceeds the agent's useful range, direct the user to support with a generated support brief - the 10 things support will need, ready to submit. This improves both user experience and support efficiency.
- **Personal AI interaction workarounds reveal the gap.** Amy has built manual logging systems for her own agent interactions: telling agents to write markdown logs, manually editing and re-injecting context when sessions go wrong, maintaining audit trails herself. "It would be amazing if there was a way for it to do it without me." The systems that should do this automatically do not exist yet.

### 8. Greg Bender's micro-interactions note

Greg raised that small broken interactions erode trust more than large failures. His example: typing in a chat input field, pressing the up arrow to correct a word, and the field jumps to a previous history item. When he returns to his original text, all the carriage returns (formatting for human readability) have been stripped.

"I'm the one you're serving, not the other way around."

Greg has more specific examples to share async. Ange requested offline follow-up. This connects to PDR-004's interaction specs and will need a micro-interaction design pass distinct from the macro flow design.

### 9. Session bookmarks / milestones (Ange)

Surfaced during the AI interaction discussion:

The group described a common pattern - hitting a failure point in an agent session, wanting to revert to an earlier state, but having no structured way to do so. Amy has built manual markdown logging to address this personally. Gabrielle referenced Figma's version history as an analogy.

Ange proposed: **named session bookmarks** - milestones in an agent session that can be referenced by name, number, event label, or timestamp. The user (or the system) can name a checkpoint; the user can later say "revert to [checkpoint name]" or "from [checkpoint] forward, take this approach."

This is more precise than undo and more powerful than session history - it addresses context degradation over long sessions rather than just the most recent action.

Gabrielle confirmed this is particularly important for complex Terraform workflows where the session may span many steps and decisions.

This concept is not yet in the PDR chain. It would sit at the intersection of the audit/history surface (PDR-003 stage 9) and the interaction model in PDR-004.

### 10. Priority matrix outcome

The group aligned on the following prioritization:

**High priority:**
- Trust / context / visibility / governance cluster: ability to review, anti-patterns, contextual starting points, providing help

**High priority:**
- Proactive detection and observability

**Borderline / discussed:**
- Onboarding

**Deferred / outside high priority:**
- Visual presentation (framed as an emotional/brand question, not a feature fix - "more about emotional connection as opposed to just visual fixes")

### 11. Workshop output scope - Ange's closing framing

This is critical for how the summary is presented to leadership:

The workshop did not produce design solutions or engineering solutions. It produced categorical directions and prioritized recommendations. The next step is leadership agreeing on which initiative comes first, after which design and engineering direct the specific feature.

"Chat" keeps being raised by leadership as the proposed solution. Ange flagged this explicitly: chat needs to be validated against whether it actually solves the highest-priority problem. The summary will name this.

Greg confirmed the output feels "very categorical" and asked whether leadership expected more specificity. Ange's answer: leadership expected specific design solutions from this workshop, but that was not the right scope. The right output is categorical direction. Specificity is the next phase.

### 12. Meridian pattern validation - hypothesis confirmed

Ange's original hypothesis: Meridian patterns are not Meridian-specific innovations but common patterns that practitioners already expect from agentic systems. If the workshop could surface these patterns without seeing Meridian first, the hypothesis would be confirmed.

Result: the workshop surfaced ability to review, contextual starting points, anti-patterns, providing help, and human-in-the-loop gates independently. These are Meridian's patterns.

Ange confirmed at the close: "A lot of these things are things that Meridian does." This means the six-month shortcut is available - adopt Meridian patterns into Terraform rather than designing from scratch.

Greg and Amy had not seen Meridian live (only screenshot images). The pattern convergence happened without direct exposure.
